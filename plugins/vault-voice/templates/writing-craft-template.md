# Writing craft (Layer B — universal)

This is the universal craft kernel. It does not change with the user; it
changes with the language. Pair it with the user-specific Layer A
(`_voice_profile.md`).

## 1. Pyramid Principle (Minto)

Top-down ordering. Lead with the answer; structure supports it.

- **Single governing thought** at the top — the answer, recommendation,
  conclusion. One sentence.
- **Two-to-five supporting points** below — each supporting a piece of
  the governing thought.
- **Evidence under each supporting point** — facts, data, examples.

The pyramid is fractal: every section, paragraph, and bullet has its own
mini-pyramid. The opening sentence of any unit answers "what is this unit
about?"

## 2. BLUF — Bottom Line Up Front

The first sentence of any communication carries the most important
information. If the reader stops after sentence one, they should still
know the answer.

Anti-pattern: long context preamble before getting to the point. Cut the
preamble; the context belongs in the supporting points, not the lede.

## 3. SCQA — Situation, Complication, Question, Answer

Use this when the audience needs context before the answer makes sense.
Common in stakeholder updates, board material, and strategic memos.

- **Situation** — the stable backdrop (≤1 sentence)
- **Complication** — what changed / what's at stake (≤2 sentences)
- **Question** — the implicit ask
- **Answer** — what you propose

When the audience already knows S and C, skip them and go straight to A.
SCQA is a framing tool, not a mandatory structure.

## 4. Clarity rules

- **Active voice** by default; passive only for deliberate effect.
- **Short sentences** — avg 15-20 words; cap 35.
- **Short paragraphs** — 3-5 sentences typical; 7 maximum.
- **Concrete over abstract** — "we shipped 12 features" beats "delivered
  significant capability".
- **Verbs over nominalisations** — "decide" beats "make a decision"; "act"
  beats "take action".
- **Specific over general** — "by Friday" beats "soon"; "$3M" beats
  "significant investment".
- **No throat-clearing** — drop "I just wanted to", "I was thinking",
  "Following up on", "Per our discussion".
- **No filler phrases** — "in order to" → "to"; "due to the fact that" →
  "because"; "at this point in time" → "now".

## 5. Discourse construction

How sentences connect within a paragraph:

- **Theme-rheme** — start each sentence with familiar information (theme),
  end with new (rheme). Each sentence's rheme becomes the next theme.
- **Old before new** — ground the reader before introducing the new.
- **End focus** — the most important word in a sentence goes at the end
  (SVO close in English).
- **Cohesion** — repeat key terms; don't elegantly-vary into synonyms that
  obscure the chain.

## 6. Reader-respect

- Assume the reader is busy.
- Assume the reader is smart.
- Make the structure visible — bold key claims, use whitespace, signal
  with `1.` `2.` when sequence matters.
- Don't repeat yourself unless emphasising; trust the reader to remember
  paragraph 1 in paragraph 4.
- Don't pre-defend. Don't anticipate every objection in advance. Let the
  reader ask.

## 7. Per-language adjustments

### English

- Strict active voice; passive feels evasive.
- SVO close: most important word at sentence end.
- Hyphens vs em-dashes: em-dashes sparingly; never as default punctuation.
- Title case for headings; sentence case for bullets.

### Portuguese

- More tolerance for longer sentences (avg 18-25 words). Don't cut PT
  sentences as aggressively as EN ones.
- Active voice still default, but PT readers are less allergic to passive.
- Watch for the "-ção" hidden-verb factory ("realização do projeto" →
  "realizar o projeto").
- Formal-executive register has distinct vocabulary: "vossa", "estimado",
  "atentamente". Match the recipient's register, not your own default.

### Spanish

- Similar tolerance to PT for sentence length.
- Subjunctive use varies by region (LatAm vs Iberian); calibrate to the
  recipient.
- Active voice default; passive less stigmatised than in EN.

## 9. When to break the rules

Every rule above has cases where breaking it is correct:

- BLUF doesn't apply to literary writing or where the framing IS the point.
- Pyramid Principle doesn't apply to chronological narrative.
- Short sentences don't apply to flowing description.
- Pre-defence is sometimes correct for hostile audiences.

The rules are heuristics for prose that respects busy readers. Apply them
by default; depart from them with intent and surface the divergence.
