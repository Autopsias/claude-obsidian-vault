## Definition

Contract novation is the legal process by which the rights and obligations under a contract are transferred from one party (the original contracting entity) to a new party (the successor entity), with the consent of all parties to the contract. Novation differs from assignment: assignment transfers rights but not obligations; novation transfers both and requires all parties to agree. In an M&A context, novation is required when the acquiring company wants to step into the acquired company's contracts — simply absorbing the entity legally may not automatically transfer contracts if the contract contains change-of-control or assignment restrictions.

## Relevance to Project Atlas

BetaCo's 847 customer contracts and 23 vendor contracts were originally between BetaCo Ltd and the respective counterparties. Post-acquisition, Acme wishes to transfer these to Acme Corp UK Ltd. This requires novation — each contract must be amended to substitute Acme Corp UK Ltd for BetaCo Ltd. Three contracts contain change-of-control clauses that give the customer the right to terminate if a change of control occurs without their consent. These three contracts (Nordex, Brightway, ClearPath) represent £1.51M ARR and require direct customer engagement before the novation can proceed.

## Counter-arguments

Novation at scale (847 contracts) is operationally intensive. An alternative approach — operating BetaCo Ltd as a continuing legal entity under Acme ownership — would avoid novation but creates long-term corporate structure complexity and does not achieve the intended brand and legal consolidation. [[Anna Fischer]] and [[Sophie Andreou]] both assessed novation as the correct path.
